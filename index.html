In This Text Set

This text set includes four informational texts and a video. All deal with an aspect of how inventions solve problems.

Thinking Like an Inventor is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign Thinking Like an Inventor for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the chart below to see where specific text structures and features appear across the texts in this set.

Standards and Objectives

  • Science Standard
    Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

  • Informational Text Focus
    In this set, students will read informational texts and view an informational video to understand how compare and contrast, sequential, and problem and solution text structures, as well as text features such as section heads, captions, diagrams, and photographs, help their understanding as they read.

    Students will understand how people can design inventions to solve problems.

  • Language Objective
    Students will explain how a variety of inventions help to solve common problems.

Thinking Like an Inventor

by Layla Edwards
Informational Text - Word Count: 370   Lexile: 520L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

  • Content Objective Students will understand how inventors identify a problem and create an invention to solve the problem.

  • Language Objective Students will describe specific types of problems and the inventions that solved those problems.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a problem and solution text structure identifies problems and how they are solved.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will recognize how photographs connect to details in a text by showing what something looks like or how it works.

Summary

This text introduces the importance of inventions for solving problems. It also explores how students can follow a process to identify a problem and create an invention to fix the problem.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? Ask: How do you think Thinking Like an Inventor will help you think about the Big Question? How might an inventor solve a problem? What are some inventions that make your life easier?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words invention and improve. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What is an invention we use every day? How does an invention improve people’s lives, or make them better?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Thinking Like an Inventor includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When you observe something, you see or notice it. What is something you observed when you walked into the classroom today? How does observing things help inventors solve problems?

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a Word Web using the word inventor. When they read Thinking Like an Inventor, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to this word. A web for inventor might look like this:

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Thinking Like an Inventor is written with a problem and solution structure. Say: In this kind of text, the writer describes a problem. Then, the writer tells how it is fixed. Sometimes, a text describes more than one problem and solution. Project the text or direct students to the introduction. Say: This first part of the text describes a problem: walking into a dark room. What is the solution to this problem? (turning on the light)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Problem and Solution Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about problem and solution structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells what needs to be fixed” and “tells how to fix it”

Text Features Say: This text includes photographs that help explain the writer’s ideas. Have students preview the photos in the text. Ask: What do most of the photos show? (different inventions) What do they help you understand about the inventions or how they work?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photos help readers understand the text. For example: “help explain ideas,” “show how something works”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Thinking Like an Inventor, have them review their completed Word Webs. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from Thinking Like an Inventor to help students as they think about words such as invention, inventor, and improve.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among Thinking Like an Inventor and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold your discussion.

  • In Thinking Like an Inventor, you learned about the invention of the lightbulb. What are some inventions you’ve read about in other texts?
  • You read about how inventors use a process to find a problem and a way to solve it. What else do you know about processes or ways of doing something?
  • Think about other texts you’ve read about inventions fixing problems. What more did you learn about fixing problems from reading Thinking Like an Inventor?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Inventions and Inventors: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Thinking Like an Inventor row with something they learned about creating inventions.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What are some problems that a car solves? What about a phone?
  • What invention could make your life better? What could you invent to solve a problem?

Write About The Big Question

Tell students: Think of a problem. Then think of a kind of invention that could solve the problem. Write two or three sentences to tell about the problem and to name the invention. Draw a picture to show your invention.

National Inventors Hall of Fame

Video - 3 minutes

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

  • Content Objective Students will learn about inventors who have been honored for solving problems.

  • Language Objective Students will describe different inventions and explain how they solve problems.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a sequential structure describes or shows events in order.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs help them understand the importance of inventors and the many solutions they find to a variety of problems.

Summary

This video follows a young student as she enjoys a guided tour through the National Inventors Hall of Fame Museum in Alexandria, Virginia. The student learns about a wide variety of types of inventions, some simple and some complex.

Before Viewing

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How can we solve problems through inventions? Ask: How do you think National Inventors Hall of Fame will help you think about the Big Question? In the video, what do you think you might discover about inventions and the problems they solve?

Preview Vocabulary

Discuss the words inventions, improve, solve, and device with students. Say: A lightbulb is one example of an invention. What other inventions can you think of? How did those inventions solve a problem? Did they improve people’s lives, or make them better? A device is an invention made for a specific use, or purpose. Often, it uses technology. A phone is one example of a device. What is another device?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in National Inventors Hall of Fame includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A patent gives credit for an invention. It shows that someone was the first person to invent it. Why might an inventor want a patent? or When you observe something, you watch it closely. Why is observing an important skill for an inventor?

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a Word Web using the word invention. As they watch National Inventors Hall of Fame, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to this word. A web for invention might look like this:

During Viewing

Elements Of INFORMATIONAL Text

Text Structure Explain that the video National Inventors Hall of Fame is told with a sequential structure. Say: A sequential structure describes events in the order that they happen. In this video, you will see what happens first, next, and last when a girl visits the National Inventors Hall of Fame. Play the first ten seconds of the video. Ask: What happens first?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Sequential Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the ideas in the video. For example: “tells what happens in order”

Text Features Say: Photographs and pictures can tell more about the information in a video or help to make information clearer. Upon a second viewing, pause the video when the narrator describes the simple bandage, and direct students’ attention to the photo of an advertisement that shows the uses for the bandage. Say: This photograph helps you see what an adhesive bandage is and how it can be used. Pause the video again when it reaches the image of Abraham Lincoln and the riverboat. Ask: How does this picture help you understand what a riverboat is? Why do you think Lincoln’s invention was important?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the second column write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photos help readers understand the text. For example: “show how an invention works”

After Viewing

Vocabulary

After students have viewed National Inventors Hall of Fame, have students review their completed Word Webs. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among National Inventors Hall of Fame and texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold your discussion.

  • In National Inventors Hall of Fame, you learned about the invention of special skis to solve a problem. What are other inventions you’ve read about? How did they help to solve a problem?
  • You learned about a few different inventors and why they invented things. What else have you read or learned about inventors?
  • In Thinking Like an Inventor, you learned about how inventions can be changed or improved. How do you think the inventions in the video have been improved?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Inventions and Inventors: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the National Inventors Hall of Fame row with something they learned about solving problems through inventions.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • One invention from the video helped put rockets into space. What problem did this invention solve? What other inventions have helped with space travel?
  • The video describes a new type of skis. How could skis make life better?

Write About The Big Question

Tell students to imagine they are going to a museum that shows inventions. Have them write about two inventions they would want to see. Then, ask them to write a list of questions they would like to ask about the inventions.

Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs

by Nick Rodrigo
Informational Text - Word Count: 393   Lexile: 530L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

  • Content Objective Students will understand Chester Greenwood’s reasons for designing a pair of earmuffs and the steps he took to create the invention.

  • Language Objective Students will describe an invention and the problem it solved.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a problem and solution text structure explains how a problem was fixed.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how diagrams help to show details and clarify information in a text. Students will recognize that a diagram can show how something works.

Summary

This text explains how 15-year-old Chester Greenwood solved a common problem by creating an invention that many people still use today.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? Show students a photo of earmuffs or a pair of earmuffs and ask: What problem do you think earmuffs would solve? How does warm clothing make life better?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words design and improve. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: An inventor designs an invention by deciding how it will look or work. How could you design something to keep people warm in the winter? When you improve something, you make it better. Think about something you wrote or made. How could you improve it?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: What material did Chester use in his earmuffs? Which changes helped to improve the invention?

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a Word Web using the word design. When they read Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to this word. A web for design might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs is written with a problem and solution structure. Say: In this text, a boy thinks about a problem and finds a way to fix it. Have students preview the section titled A Cold, Cold Problem. Say: What problem is the writer describing? How do you think Chester can solve it?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Problem and Solution Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about problem and solution structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “describes a problem” and “tells how a problem was solved”

Text Features Say: Diagrams are special features. They are images, or pictures, that can show the parts of something or the way something works. Direct students’ attention to the diagram of the earmuffs. Point out one part of the diagram, and have students read the label with you. Say: The labels name parts of the diagram. Continue with the remaining labels. Ask: Why do you think the author included a diagram with this text? Guide students to understand that the diagram helps them recognize the parts of the earmuffs that are discussed in the text.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Diagram.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how the diagram helps readers understand the text. For example: “shows the parts of an invention,” “shows how something works”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs, have them review their completed Word Webs. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs to help students as they think about words such as design, solution, and improve.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold your discussion.

  • In Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs, you learned about the invention of special earmuffs to solve a problem. What else have you read about how inventions solve problems?
  • Chester improved his earmuffs by adding parts or using better parts. What other inventions have been improved over time? What have you read about these inventions?
  • In Thinking Like an Inventor, you learned about a process that inventors use for solving problems. What are the steps in the process? How did Chester use this process to make his earmuffs?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Inventions and Inventors: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Chester Greenwood’s Earmuffs row with something they learned about solving problems using inventions.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Chester Greenwood developed his earmuffs to solve a problem. What problem did they solve?
  • How did his solution help other people, too?
  • Can you think of a way to make earmuffs better?

Write About the Big Question

Say: Think about people’s hands getting cold in icy weather. Write four steps for an invention that will help keep hands warm. Draw a diagram with labels to show your invention. This invention can be something completely new, or it can be an invention that makes something we already have better.

Inventions from Space Travel

by Ryan McCauley
Informational Text - Word Count: 496   Lexile: 600L

Instructional Focus

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how section heads can help organize the information in a text.

Clean Energy Inventions

by Karan Singer
Informational Text - Word Count: 535   Lexile: 720L

  • Science Standard Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize the differences between renewable and nonrenewable energy sources, as well as problems and solutions related to these energy sources.

  • Language Objective Students will explain how inventions help people use clean energy sources to solve problems.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure shows how things are alike and how they are different.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how section headings tell them something important about the information they are preparing to read.

Summary

This informational text explains how inventions use renewable energy sources to help solve environmental problems or challenges.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? Say: Solar power and wind power are examples of clean energy. What inventions might help people use this energy? How do you think clean energy might solve problems?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words energy, inventions, and renewable. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What are some examples of energy? How do inventions help people get energy? When a resource is renewable, we can always get more of it. Why is it important to use renewable energy?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Clean Energy Inventions includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Solar energy comes from the sun. Why do you think solar energy is a renewable resource?

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a Word Web using the word energy. As they read Clean Energy Inventions, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to this word. A web for energy might look like this:

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Clean Energy Inventions is written using a compare and contrast structure. Say: In this structure, the writer writes to show how things are alike and the way they are different. Direct students to screen 7 (Printable Text p. x). Point out the word unlike. Say: This word shows that the writer is contrasting two things, or telling how they are different. What is the writer contrasting? (renewable and nonrenewable energy)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the ideas in the text. For example: “tells how things are the same and different”

Text Features Say: You have read many texts that have phrases in bold print at the beginning of a section. These are called headings. They tell you what you will read about. Point out the bold heading Problems with Nonrenewable Energy. Ask: What does this heading tell you about the information in this part of the text? Guide students to understand that the heading tells them they will read information about a kind of energy that does not work well.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with how headings help them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tell what a section will be about”

After Reading

Vocabulary

Encourage students to make connections among Clean Energy Inventions and other texts they have read on related subjects. You may wish to provide sentence frames to scaffold your discussion.

  • In Clean Energy Inventions, how do the inventions solve problems? What else have you read about inventions that solve problems?
  • Clean Energy Inventions describes solar panels. What else have you read about this invention?
  • Remember what you read in Thinking Like an Inventor. How do you think people thought of the inventions in this text?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Inventions and Inventors: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Clean Energy Inventions row with something they learned about solving problems through inventions.

  • How did the invention of the wind turbine help to solve a problem?
  • Why are solar panels helpful in solving problems?
  • How does clean energy make people’s lives better?
  • What are some other problems related to energy? How can they be solved?

Write About the Big Question

Tell students: Imagine that you are a news reporter who has interviewed the inventor of a solar panel. Write a short article to tell why the inventor wanted to make this invention.

Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read and viewed some or all of the pieces in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading and viewing in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Have students review their Word Webs. Ask them to choose one word and draw an illustration that helps them understand the meaning of the word. Tell them to write a sentence that includes the vocabulary word to tell about the illustration. Have them repeat for three more words.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion related to the Big Question: How can inventions solve problems or make life better? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the pieces they have read and viewed in the text set, using as many of the words from their Word Webs as they can.

  • What did you already know about inventions and inventors?
  • You’ve read many texts about inventions and the problems they solved. What did you learn about how inventions solve problems? How did the inventions improve people’s lives?
  • What else would you like to know about inventions?
  • What texts or videos were most useful in helping you answer the Big Question?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding.

PROJECT: You Be the Inventor!

Tell students they will be writing about inventions they would like to create. Tell them this may be a completely new invention or an improvement on an invention that already exists. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

Display the following steps and read them aloud. Ask students if they have questions about any of the steps. Then ask them to number and follow these steps as they write about their inventions on their own paper:

1. Tell about the problem.

2. Learn more about the problem by talking to classmates, teachers, and family members. You may also read classroom sources to learn more. Write to tell the information you have learned.

3. Write the plan you will use to fix the problem. This is your solution.

4. Tell how you would test your solution to see if it works.

5. Create one or more drawings to show your invention.

Encourage students to refer to their previous Write About the Big Question activities as they complete the project. Invite them to share their completed projects in small groups or with the whole class.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
